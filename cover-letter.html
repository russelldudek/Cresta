ToColin Jenkins and the Cresta AI Deployment team
Re: AI Deployment Manager

Cresta's job description draws the right line: shipping AI is only half the story. The other half is changing the operating state.

That distinction is why this role fits the through-line of my career. I have repeatedly worked where technical capability meets operational consequence—launching customer-backward operations, connecting engineering and production, turning field and customer signals into visible work, and building the routines that help people adopt a new way of operating.

At Vape-Jet, I lead an AI-first operating transformation spanning production, purchasing, quality, customer success, support, engineering issue flow, ERP, CRM, collaboration systems, telemetry, and AI-assisted workflows. The work is not “add AI.” It is clarify the decision, connect the signal, design the handoff, train the team, and make the result inspectable. At Amazon, I led launch and execution in a 24/7 customer-backward environment and built the operating mechanisms, escalation paths, and standard work that turn an ambitious promise into repeatable delivery. Earlier at Compunetics, I connected engineering, manufacturing, quality, customer delivery, and high-reliability technical programs.

I would not claim a decade of conventional CCaaS implementation experience. I would bring deployment-shaped operating leadership, technical fluency, a consultative customer stance, and the humility to learn Cresta's products and delivery system from the people who built them. My adjacent experience is strongest exactly where enterprise AI deployments tend to become difficult: readiness, ownership, adoption, evidence, and cross-functional momentum.

Cresta's current moment makes that work especially compelling. As the platform and Conductor increase product velocity, the deployment organization has an opportunity to make customer confidence and measurable value scale just as quickly. My candidate vision proposes an Activation Boundary: four conditions—Outcome, Readiness, Adoption, and Evidence—held together by clear ownership. It is a hypothesis for discovery, not a claim about Cresta's internal process, and it demonstrates how I would approach complexity with structure and restraint.

I would welcome the chance to learn how your team currently defines a healthy deployment, where enterprise initiatives most often stall, and what Colin and the team need this hire to make easier over the next year.

Carpe diem,Russell Dudek
